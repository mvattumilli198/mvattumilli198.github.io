Executive Summary

What this project does in 200–300 words.

The Horizon Retention & Success Hub is an early-noticing platform that pulls together information that usually lives in separate university systems: academic records, course enrollments, learning management system (LMS) usage, attendance logs, and financial indicators. The project uses a fully synthetic dataset of about 5,000 students over four academic terms, designed to mimic realistic behavior without exposing real student data.

On top of this data foundation, we trained a multi-class XGBoost model that predicts whether a student in a given term is at Low, Medium, or High academic risk. The model reaches roughly 92% accuracy on held-out test data. Its predictions are written into a riskscores table so that the backend can query risk tiers just like any other column.

Around the model, we built a full application stack: a Spring Boot backend with JWT-based authentication and REST APIs, and a React frontend with separate advisor and student experiences. Advisors can scan high-risk students, drill into longitudinal profiles, and capture advising notes. Students see a compact view of their GPA trend, attendance, engagement, and a plain-language explanation of their risk tier.

Although the data is artificial, the overall workflow—data model, machine learning pipeline, APIs, and dashboards—is intentionally close to commercial student-success platforms used in higher education.

Problem Context & Stakeholder Perspectives

Why this problem matters

Universities are under pressure to improve retention and graduation rates, but information about students is scattered across grade books, LMS logs, attendance systems, financial aid, and advising notes. In many cases, advisors only notice that a student is in trouble once they are failing multiple courses or have stopped showing up—at which point it is much harder to change the trajectory.

The core problem is the absence of a unified, intelligence-supported view that combines academic, behavioral, and financial signals into something advisors and students can act on.

Stakeholders

Academic Advisors

Need an integrated view that answers questions like: “Who is most at risk right now?”, “What has changed across recent terms?”, and “What interventions have already been tried?”

Students

See grades in one place, billing in another, and attendance feedback somewhere else. A single, honest dashboard helps them understand where they stand and why.

University Leadership

Care about big-picture patterns—by major, term, or group—and whether advising resources are aligned with need. Our architecture leaves room for future institutional-level views.

Research & Insights

Student success analytics Early warning systems Educational data mining

What the literature says

  • Early signals matter: drops in attendance and LMS activity often appear before GPA declines.
  • Single metrics (like GPA alone) are weak predictors; combining performance, engagement, and financial stress leads to better models.
  • Dashboards must be actionable, not just pretty—users need thresholds, flags, and next steps.
  • Risk scores are sensitive artifacts; fairness, transparency, and interpretation are part of the design, not an afterthought.

How this shaped our design

  • Features combine GPA, attendance, LMS behavior, and financial indicators instead of focusing on one dimension.
  • Advisor views emphasize High-risk students by default and add filters for GPA, risk tier, and engagement.
  • Student views present risk tier in everyday language plus clear explanations of what might help.

Proposed Solution

Advisor & Student Portal (React)

A single web application with role-based views. Advisors see summary metrics, risk-ranked student lists, detailed profiles, and an advising notes workflow. Students see their own KPIs, trends, and tailored suggestions.

Spring Boot Backend

REST APIs for login, advisor dashboard, student dashboard, risk retrieval, and notes. Uses JWT tokens and role checks so advisors and students only see what they are allowed to see.

XGBoost Risk Model

Multi-class classifier trained on engineered features (GPA, attendance, LMS usage, financial data). Produces Low / Medium / High risk tiers per student–term and stores them in the riskscores table.

Project Charter

Goals & Scope

  • Design an end-to-end early-alert prototype for student success.
  • Integrate synthetic data from 11 tables into a consistent PostgreSQL schema.
  • Train and evaluate a multi-class model that predicts risk tiers with meaningful accuracy.
  • Deliver working advisor and student dashboards backed by real APIs.

Out of Scope & Success Metrics

  • Out of scope: production deployment, real student data, full institutional reporting.
  • Technical success: API response < 300 ms, reasonable load times, ≈ 92% model accuracy.
  • UX success: advisors can identify high-risk students within a few seconds; students understand their risk tier and drivers.

Architecture & Data Design

Enterprise Architecture Overview

The system follows a three-tier pattern: React frontend, Spring Boot API, and PostgreSQL database, with an offline Python ML pipeline that enriches the data warehouse with risk scores.

ER Diagram & Data Flow

The schema includes tables for students, courses, enrollments, term GPAs, LMS events, attendance, financial aid, ML risk scores, and advising notes. Data flows from CSV files into PostgreSQL, then into a feature table in Python. After the model assigns risk tiers, predictions are imported back into the riskscores table for use by the backend.

Dashboard & Data Analysis

Advisor Dashboard

  • Metric cards: total students, High-risk count, average term GPA, average attendance.
  • Table of students with major, current term GPA, attendance, risk tier, and quick filters.
  • Profile drill-down: term-by-term GPA, course list, engagement and attendance summaries, current risk tier, and reasoning.

Student Dashboard

  • Shows cumulative GPA, current term GPA, attendance, and engagement.
  • GPA trend chart across recent terms helps students see whether they are improving or slipping.
  • Recommendation panel translates the data into simple next steps (for example, meet with advisor, increase LMS engagement).

Key Insights from the Data

  • Students with consistently low attendance and low LMS engagement almost always fall into the Medium or High risk tiers.
  • Some students with reasonable term GPAs still show elevated risk due to sharp declines in engagement, which the dashboard helps surface early.
  • Viewing risk by major and term highlights pockets where extra advising capacity could have the highest payoff.

Ethical & Social Considerations

Fairness & Bias

Risk scores can influence how advisors perceive and prioritize students. In a real deployment, the model would need to be regularly audited for disparate impact across demographics such as gender, residency, or first-generation status, and risk tiers should be treated as support signals, not labels of student capability.

Privacy & FERPA

Our prototype uses only synthetic data. In production, strong access control, encryption, and compliance with FERPA and institutional policies would be required. Role-based access in our design (advisor vs. student) is one step toward that.

Interpretability

We intentionally present simple metrics (GPA, attendance, engagement) and a discrete risk tier instead of a mysterious score. Future iterations could add explainable AI techniques (e.g., SHAP) so advisors can see which factors are driving a student’s risk classification.

Lessons Learned & Team Reflection

Building the Horizon Retention & Success Hub gave the team a realistic end-to-end view of a data product: from synthetic data generation and schema design to feature engineering, model training, backend APIs, and frontend dashboards.

On the technical side, we deepened our understanding of Spring Boot, JWT-based authentication, and secure frontend–backend integration. On the analytics side, we worked with multi-class classification, evaluation metrics, and the trade-offs between accuracy and interpretability.

As a team, we also experienced how important early alignment is—agreeing on architecture, shared vocabulary, and user stories before diving into code. Iterating on dashboard design and reviewing each other’s work helped move the project from a collection of scripts to a coherent product.
