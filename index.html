Executive Summary

What this project does and why it matters.

The Horizon Retention & Success Hub is an early-noticing platform that pulls together data that usually lives in separate university systems. It combines academic records, course enrollments, learning management system (LMS) activity, attendance logs, and financial indicators for about 5,000 synthetic students across four terms.

On top of this data foundation, we trained a multi-class XGBoost model that predicts whether a student in a given term is at Low, Medium, or High academic risk. The model reaches roughly 92 percent accuracy on held-out test data. Its predictions are written into a riskscores table so that the backend can read risk tiers just like any other column.

Around this model, we built a full application stack. The Spring Boot backend exposes REST endpoints with JWT-based authentication and role checks. The React frontend provides separate experiences for advisors and students. Advisors scan high-risk students, drill into term-by-term profiles, and log advising notes. Students see their GPA trends, attendance, engagement metrics, and a plain-language explanation of their risk tier.

Even though the dataset is synthetic, the workflow from data model to dashboards is intentionally close to commercial student-success platforms that many universities use today.

Problem Context & Stakeholder Perspectives

Why this problem exists

Universities are under pressure to improve retention and graduation rates, but information about students is scattered across grade books, LMS logs, attendance systems, financial aid records, and advising notes. In many cases, advisors only realize that a student is in trouble once the student is already failing courses or has stopped attending.

The core issue is the lack of a unified, intelligent view that combines academic, behavioral, and financial signals into something advisors and students can act on in time.

Who is affected

Academic Advisors

Need an integrated view that answers questions like "Who is most at risk right now?", "What changed over recent terms?" and "What interventions are already on record?".

Students

See grades in one system, tuition and aid somewhere else, and attendance feedback in another. A single, honest dashboard helps them understand where they stand and what to focus on.

University Leadership

Care about patterns by major, term, and student group, and whether advising resources match the areas of highest need. Our architecture leaves room to add institutional views on top of the existing APIs.

Research & Insights

Student success analytics Early warning systems Educational data mining

What recent work shows

  • Drops in attendance and LMS activity often appear before GPA drops, so they can be useful early warning signals.
  • Single metrics like GPA on its own are weak predictors. Models work better when they combine performance, engagement, and financial stress.
  • Dashboards that only display data without thresholds, flags, or next steps are less likely to change advisor behavior.
  • Risk scores are sensitive. Fairness, transparency, and interpretation should be treated as core design concerns and not just technical details.

How this shaped our design

  • We engineered features that combine GPA, attendance, LMS usage, and financial indicators instead of focusing on one dimension.
  • The advisor dashboard emphasizes high-risk students and provides filters for GPA, risk tier, and engagement.
  • The student view describes risk tiers in simple language and pairs them with concrete suggestions instead of only showing a label.

Proposed Solution

Advisor & Student Portal

A single web application with role-based views. Advisors see summary metrics, risk-ranked student lists, detailed profiles, and an advising notes workflow. Students see their own KPIs, trends, and tailored suggestions.

Spring Boot Backend

REST APIs for login, advisor dashboard, student dashboard, risk retrieval, and advising notes. JWT tokens and role checks ensure that advisors and students only see the data they are allowed to see.

XGBoost Risk Model

A multi-class classifier trained on engineered features such as GPA, attendance, LMS usage, and financial data. It produces Low, Medium, or High risk tiers for each student-term and writes them into the riskscores table.

Project Charter

Goals & Scope

  • Design an end-to-end early-alert prototype for student success.
  • Integrate synthetic data from 11 tables into a consistent PostgreSQL schema.
  • Train and evaluate a multi-class model that predicts risk tiers with useful accuracy.
  • Deliver working advisor and student dashboards backed by real APIs.

Out of scope & success metrics

  • Out of scope: production deployment, real student data, and full institution-level reporting.
  • Technical success: API responses under 300 ms, smooth dashboard load times, and about 92 percent model accuracy.
  • Experience success: advisors can identify high-risk students within a few seconds of logging in, and students can understand their risk tier and the main drivers.

Architecture & Data Design

Enterprise architecture overview

The system follows a three-tier pattern with a React frontend, Spring Boot API layer, and PostgreSQL database. An offline Python pipeline builds features, trains the XGBoost model, and writes risk predictions into the database.

ER diagram & data flow

The schema includes tables for students, courses, enrollments, term GPAs, LMS events, attendance, financial aid, ML risk scores, and advising notes. Data flows from CSV files into PostgreSQL, then into a feature table in Python. After the model assigns risk tiers, predictions are imported back into the riskscores table so that the backend can serve them through the API.

Dashboard & Data Analysis

Advisor dashboard

  • Summary cards show total students, high-risk count, average term GPA, and average attendance.
  • A table lists students with major, current term GPA, attendance, risk tier, and quick filters.
  • Clicking a row opens a profile with term-by-term GPA, current courses, engagement and attendance summaries, and a current risk tier.

Student dashboard

  • Shows cumulative GPA, current term GPA, attendance, and engagement scores in one place.
  • A GPA trend chart helps students see whether they are improving, flat, or slipping across terms.
  • A recommendation panel turns the data into simple next steps such as meeting with an advisor or increasing LMS engagement.

Key insights from the data

  • Students with consistently low attendance and low LMS engagement almost always fall into the Medium or High risk tiers.
  • Some students with reasonable term GPAs still show elevated risk because their engagement has dropped sharply, which the dashboard surfaces earlier than grades alone.
  • Looking at risk by major and term highlights pockets where additional advising capacity could have the highest effect.

Ethical & Social Considerations

Fairness and bias

Risk scores can shape how advisors perceive students. In a real deployment, the model should be audited regularly for differences across groups such as gender, residency status, or first-generation status. Scores should be treated as support signals and never as labels of student ability.

Privacy and FERPA

Our prototype uses only synthetic data. In production, strict access control, encryption, and compliance with FERPA and institutional policy would be required. Role-based access in our design is one step in that direction, with different views for advisors and students.

Interpretability

We intentionally present simple metrics and a discrete risk tier instead of an opaque number. Future versions could integrate explainable AI techniques so that advisors can see which features contributed most to a given student's risk classification.

Lessons Learned & Team Reflection

Building the Horizon Retention & Success Hub gave our team a realistic end-to-end view of a data product. We went from synthetic data generation and schema design to feature engineering, model training, backend APIs, and user-facing dashboards.

On the technical side, we deepened our understanding of Spring Boot, JWT-based authentication, and secure frontend to backend integration. On the analytical side, we worked with multi-class classification, evaluation metrics, and the tradeoffs between accuracy and interpretability.

As a team, we also saw how important early alignment is. Agreeing on architecture, shared vocabulary, and user stories before writing code helped avoid rework. Iterating on dashboard designs and reviewing each other's work turned a set of scripts and mockups into a coherent product.
