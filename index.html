Home & Executive Summary

Project Overview

The Horizon Retention & Success Hub is a web-based early-alert platform designed to help a university identify and support students who are at risk of struggling academically. The system brings together fragmented data from multiple sources (grades, LMS activity, attendance, financial indicators, and advising notes) into a single, actionable view for academic advisors and students.

Using a synthetic dataset of about 5,000 students across four terms, we engineered features from 11 tables and trained a multi-class XGBoost model to classify each student-term record as Low, Medium, or High risk. The model reached roughly 92% accuracy on held-out test data. These predictions are stored in PostgreSQL and exposed through a Spring Boot API.

On the frontend, a React application provides separate dashboards for advisors and students. Advisors can quickly scan high-risk students, drill into a profile, and view or add advising notes. Students can see their own GPA trends, attendance and engagement summaries, and simple recommendations. While all data is synthetic, the workflow mirrors real student-success platforms and can be adapted to real institutional contexts.

Problem Context & Stakeholder Perspectives

Problem Overview

Universities are under pressure to improve student retention and graduation rates, but the information needed to spot students who are slipping behind is scattered across multiple systems. Advisors may see grades in one interface, attendance in another, LMS activity in a different portal, and financial indicators elsewhere. Without a unified, intelligent view, at-risk students may only be noticed after failing multiple courses or disengaging completely.

Stakeholders

  • Academic Advisors: need an integrated view of each student’s performance, engagement, and history of interventions to decide whom to reach out to first and how to support them.
  • Students: often see information in fragments; they benefit from a clear, single dashboard that shows where they stand and what they can do next.
  • University Leadership: cares about trends by major, term, and risk segment; while our prototype focuses on advisor and student views, the architecture is ready for institutional analytics.

Research & Insights

Literature Highlights

  • Early drops in attendance and LMS activity often precede GPA declines.
  • Combining multiple dimensions (GPA, engagement, financial factors) usually outperforms single-metric approaches.
  • Early-warning systems are most effective when they lead to clear, actionable workflows for advisors.
  • Ethical design and transparency around risk scores are critical to avoid stigmatizing students.

Insights Used in Our Design

  • We engineered features from GPA, attendance, LMS activity, and financial variables.
  • The advisor dashboard highlights High-risk students and supports filtering and drill-down.
  • The student view focuses on simple KPIs and clear explanations of why they are in a given risk tier.

Proposed Solution & Project Charter

Solution Overview

The Horizon Retention & Success Hub is a full-stack web application that combines a React frontend, a Spring Boot backend with JWT authentication, a PostgreSQL database with 11 tables, and an offline XGBoost model that generates student-term risk predictions.

  • Advisor Portal: risk-tier dashboard, student details, and advising notes.
  • Student Portal: personalized dashboard with GPA trends and recommendations.
  • ML Risk Engine: multi-class XGBoost model predicting Low/Medium/High risk.

Project Charter (Summary)

  • Goal: Help advisors identify at-risk students early and support data-informed interventions.
  • Scope: Synthetic data, predictive model, web dashboards, and basic note-taking workflow.
  • Out of Scope: Real student data, full SIS/LMS integration, and production security hardening.
  • Key Stakeholders: advisors, students, academic support staff, and institutional leaders.
  • Deliverables: dataset, trained model, backend APIs, dashboards, written report, and portfolio website.

Architecture & Data Design

System Architecture

The system follows a three-tier design: a React frontend, a Spring Boot REST API, and a PostgreSQL database. An offline Python pipeline (Pandas + XGBoost) generates risk scores that are imported into the riskscores table.

Machine Learning Pipeline

We load and join tables from the synthetic Excel workbook, engineer student-term features, and train an XGBoost classifier to predict risk tiers. The model is evaluated on a stratified 80/20 split and then used to score all 20,000 student-term rows.

ER Diagram & Data Model

The PostgreSQL schema includes entities for students, courses, terms, enrollments, grades, attendance, LMS events, term GPAs, financial aid, risk scores, and advising notes. This design makes it straightforward to reconstruct a student’s history and compute features for modeling.

Key Insights

  • High-risk students tend to show both lower attendance and lower LMS engagement than peers, even before grades drop sharply.
  • Students with higher financial stress indicators also appear more frequently in Medium and High risk tiers, suggesting the value of pairing academic and financial support.
  • The dashboard makes these patterns visible at a glance so advisors can prioritize outreach and track whether risk levels improve over time.

Ethical & Social Considerations

Fairness & Bias

In a real deployment, risk models must be evaluated for fairness across demographic groups, such as gender, residency status, or first-generation status. Risk scores should be treated as signals for support, not as labels of student potential or worth.

Privacy & Transparency

Our prototype uses synthetic data only. For actual student data, strict controls, audit logs, and compliance with FERPA would be essential. We deliberately expose relatively simple metrics (GPA, attendance, engagement, and risk tier) to keep the system understandable to both advisors and students.

Lessons Learned & Team Reflection

Technical Lessons

  • Designing a relational schema upfront made feature engineering and queries much easier later.
  • Integrating Spring Boot, JWT authentication, and a React frontend required careful coordination of routes and roles.
  • Working with a multi-class XGBoost model reinforced our understanding of train–test splits, evaluation metrics, and model persistence.

Process & Collaboration

  • We learned to align early on project scope and user stories to avoid rework.
  • Sharing a consistent understanding of advisor vs. student experience helped keep the UI coherent.
  • Breaking work into backend, data, ML, and frontend tracks allowed us to move in parallel and then integrate.
